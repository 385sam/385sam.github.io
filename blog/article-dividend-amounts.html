How Much Capital for $500–$2,000/Month in Dividends?

📅 April 2026 ⏱️ 6 min 👤 DCS team
Capital PlanningIncome MathGoals

Income targets sound simple (“I want $1,000/month”), but plans break when you ignore net yield (after taxes & fees), inflation, and the safety buffer you actually need. This guide gives you fast benchmarks for $500–$2,000/month, then shows how to model your exact scenario in DCSimulator.

Core formula: required capital ≈ (target monthly income × 12) ÷ net yield. Net yield is what you keep after taxes and fund fees.

Net yield is the number that matters

Gross yield is not what funds your spending. DCSimulator converts gross yield into net yield using:

Net yield ≈ gross yield × (1 − tax rate) − expense ratio

Example: a 3.5% gross yield with 15% dividend tax and 0.20% fund fees becomes roughly 3.5% × 0.85 − 0.20% ≈ 2.78% net. Planning off 3.5% would understate required capital.

Use DCSimulator to get your personalized capital range

Benchmarks are useful, but your plan should be built on your inputs:

  • Desired monthly income: enter the after-tax amount you want to live on.
  • Time horizon: when you need the income (5, 10, 20+ years).
  • Dividend Yield, Annual growth, and Growth volatility: test conservative vs optimistic assumptions.
  • Tax rate and Expense ratio: these directly reduce net yield.
  • Inflation: the simulator increases your income target over time to preserve purchasing power.

Pick the percentile you plan around

DCSimulator shows percentiles for required capital. Lower numbers are “favorable”; higher numbers are “safer.” For most users:

  • P50: median requirement (good context).
  • P75: prudent planning anchor (recommended baseline).
  • P90: safety-first stress test (bigger buffer).

If you are still accumulating: use monthly savings

Enter your monthly savings to see a projected savings amount at your horizon and the additional capital needed beyond that projection. This is the fastest way to turn an abstract target into an executable plan.

Bottom line

Use the table to sanity-check your target, then build your plan on net yield + inflation + a conservative percentile (P75/P90). That’s the difference between “possible on paper” and “durable in real life.”
