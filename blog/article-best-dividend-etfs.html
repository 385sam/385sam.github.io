Best Dividend ETFs in 2026: SCHD, DGRO, VYM, and JEPI

📅 April 2026 ⏱️ 6 min 👤 DCS team
ETF AnalysisComparison

For most investors, dividend ETFs are the cleanest way to build income: broad diversification, transparent rules, and far less single-stock risk than assembling a portfolio one company at a time.

The “best” dividend ETF is the one that matches your objective — and that you can model realistically. That’s exactly what DCSimulator is for.

How to model an ETF (or ETF mix) in DCSimulator:
1) Set your after-tax monthly income target and horizon
2) Estimate portfolio-weighted dividend yield, dividend growth, and expense ratio
3) Enter your dividend tax rate and inflation
4) Run the simulation and plan around P75 (check P90)

Quick comparison (inputs that matter in the simulator)

ETF Role Typical yield Expense ratio Simulator notes
SCHD Core ~3%–4% ~0.06% Balanced yield + quality; good baseline inputs
DGRO Growth tilt ~2%–3% ~0.08% Lower yield, often higher dividend growth; long-horizon friendly
VYM Broad dividend exposure ~2.5%–3.5% ~0.06% Broader and often less concentrated than SCHD
JEPI Income sleeve Varies (often higher) ~0.35% Distribution mix is different; be conservative on “growth” and stress-test taxes

SCHD: best all-around core holding

Typical yield: around 3% to 4% depending on market conditions
Expense ratio: 0.06%
Focus: U.S. companies with durable dividends and strong fundamentals

SCHD is often the default choice because it balances quality, yield, and cost. It is not the highest-yielding fund, but it is one of the most practical core holdings for long-term dividend investors.

Simulator tip: Use SCHD-like inputs as a baseline, then run a stress test with slightly lower yield or higher taxes to see how much buffer you need at P75/P90.

DGRO: dividend growth with a quality tilt

Typical yield: around 2% to 3%
Expense ratio: 0.08%
Focus: companies with consistent dividend growth

DGRO is useful if you care more about raising income over time than maximizing income today. It often suits investors who are still accumulating and want a portfolio that can grow dividends faster than inflation.

Simulator tip: Try a lower yield with higher dividend growth and compare required capital at 5 years vs 20 years. This shows when growth starts to matter.

VYM: broad high-dividend exposure

Typical yield: around 2.5% to 3.5%
Expense ratio: 0.06%
Focus: a wider set of high-dividend U.S. companies

VYM is broader and usually less concentrated than SCHD. That can be helpful if you want a more market-like dividend exposure instead of a narrower screen.

JEPI: higher current income, different trade-offs

Typical yield: often higher, but it changes
Expense ratio: 0.35%
Focus: equity income using covered calls and a large-cap stock portfolio

JEPI is not a pure dividend ETF. A meaningful part of its distribution can come from option income. That can be useful for current income, but it also means you should not assume the same growth profile (or tax profile) as a dividend-growth ETF.

Simulator tip: Be conservative: model lower “annual growth”, include the higher fee, and consider whether your tax rate on distributions is higher than qualified dividends.

How to model a blended portfolio

If you combine ETFs, approximate your portfolio inputs using weighted averages (for yield and expense ratio) and a conservative growth assumption. Then let the simulator show you the capital range and the P75/P90 buffers.

Bottom line

Start with a low-cost core (SCHD/VYM style), add a growth tilt (DGRO style) if your horizon is long, and treat higher-income products (JEPI style) as optional sleeves that require stricter stress tests.
